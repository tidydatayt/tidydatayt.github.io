10 Comparing Cohen d and Pearson r

10.1 Effect size - Cohen’s

Cohen is used to quantify the size of the difference between two groups, taking into account the variability within each group.

10.1.1 Population

\[d = \frac{\mu_2 -\mu_1}{\sigma}\] ### Sample

We can use a sample to estimate the by replacing with sample means and sample standard deviation.

\[d = \frac{m_2 -m_1}{s_{pooled}}=\frac{m_2 -m_1}{\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2+s_2^2}{2}}}\]

\(s_1\) is the sample standard deviation for group 1, whereas \(s_2\) is the sample standard deviation for group 2.

10.2 Effect Size - Pearson Product Moment

The Pearson product-moment correlation coefficient, often referred to as the Pearson correlation coefficient (\(\rho\)) , quantifies the strength of a linear relationship between two variables.

10.2.1 Population

\[\rho =\frac{cov(X,Y)}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y}\]

10.2.2 Sample

We can use a sample correlation coefficient, namely , to estimate population correlation coefficient \(\rho\).

\[r_{xy} =\frac{cov(x,y)}{s_xs_y}=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y})}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i-\bar{x})^2}\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i-\bar{y})^2}}\]

Cohen’s can assume values ranging from 0 to infinity (i.e., can be greater than 1), whereas Pearson’s is confined to a range between -1 and 1.

Note that, based on my best understanding (not sure), Cohen’s can be negative as well, depending on how to define \(m_1\) and \(m_2\) and which order to put them.
